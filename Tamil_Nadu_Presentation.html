Project Objectives

  1. Estimate Allocation: Per-student funds released under Samagra Shiksha (Last 3 years).
  2. Estimate Expenditure: Actual per-student spending across 38 TN districts.
  3. Compile Outcomes: Analyze NAS 2021, PARAKH 2024, and SLAS 2025 achievement results.
  4. Discuss Relationships: Evaluate the correlation between financial input and educational output.

1. Financial Estimates (2021 - 2025)

Tracking Samagra Shiksha Funding

  • Methodology: Total District Grants Released ÷ UDISE+ Govt & Aided Enrollment = Per-Student Expenditure.
  • The Baseline (2021-2023): Stable central/state funding averaging ~₹8,500 per student across districts, heavily skewed toward teacher salaries and infrastructure.
  • The 2024 Anomaly: Due to the withholding of central Samagra Shiksha funds, the 2024-2025 estimated expenditure dropped to ~₹4,500 per student, forcing reliance on state budgets.

2. Learning Achievements

NAS 2021 → PARAKH 2024 → SLAS 2025

  • Language Recovery: Literacy scores show signs of pandemic recovery. Grade 5 Language LO averages hit 76%, and Grade 3 Language LO is recovering.
  • The STEM Crisis: Foundational numeracy is struggling. Grade 8 Mathematics averages a dismal ~38%, and Science hovers around ~37% state-wide.
  • The Geographic Divide: Southern/Coastal districts (Kanniyakumari, Madurai) consistently outperform highly urbanized/industrial districts (Chennai, Coimbatore).

4. Discussion: The Relationship

Does spending more equal better learning?
The correlation is weak-to-moderate.

  • The Urban Paradox: High-spending urban districts (Chennai) often show lower learning outcomes compared to lower-spending rural/southern districts.
  • Quality over Quantity: Samagra Shiksha funds are largely absorbed by infrastructure (repairs, IT). This capital expenditure does not translate to immediate pedagogical gains like foundational numeracy.
  • Socio-Economic Multipliers: Top-performing districts rely on historical socio-educational foundations and community involvement, acting as a multiplier for any state funds received.

Conclusion


  • While adequate funding is a prerequisite for functioning schools, the volume of per-student expenditure alone does not guarantee competency.

  • Policy must shift focus from simply releasing funds to ensuring those funds drive targeted pedagogical interventions in struggling foundational subjects like Mathematics.
